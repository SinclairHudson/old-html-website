Project:

DIY Portable Speaker

During my Grade 12 Year, I had the wonderful opportunity to work with an ex-Hydro One electrical engineer, James Whatley, in my Computer Engineering 12 Class. We learned and then built voltage regulation circuits, amperage regulations circuits, full-wave rectifiers, but we were short on time at the end of the year. Eager to learn more, I talked a bit with my instructor and we agreed to meet for a few days in the early summer to work on another project: the portable speaker.

What I Learned:

  • Circuit Theory and Electrical Engineering
  • Troubleshooting malfunctioning circuits with an oscilloscope
  • Designing and printing custom 3D plastic models

How It Works:

The speaker has a standard 3.5mm headphone jack as an input. There is also a circuit to allow for a microphone to be attached, but I opted to leave out the mic in the final build. The back of the speaker has an on/off switch, and there’s an indicator LED on the front. Since there is only one speaker, there is a switch on the back of the build that allows the user to switch between the left channel, right channel, and microphone as an input. After that, the signal is filtered and amplified to drive the speaker itself. Once the electronics were complete, I measured all the components and drew up a custom housing for the project in Autodesk Inventor. After that, I borrowed a friend’s 3D printer and printed the housing to create a nice aesthetic package for the electronics. I’m quite happy with the result; the sound quality isn’t great, but it’s extremely satisfying to play music from your own speaker.
